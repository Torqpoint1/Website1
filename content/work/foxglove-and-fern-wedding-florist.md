## The project

Foxglove & Fern dressed a marquee wedding outside Cirencester — a ceremony arch, fifteen tablescapes, bouquets and buttonholes, all in a loose, garden-gathered style. A full day's work, gone by the next morning. The kind of thing that should book the next ten weddings — and usually books none, because nobody writes it up.

## The problem

Wedding flowers are the definition of fleeting: weeks of planning, one perfect day, then it's packed down and forgotten. Couples choose a florist almost entirely on seeing previous weddings — so a florist who doesn't present their work properly is invisible at the exact moment a couple is choosing. Foxglove & Fern's best work was vanishing the morning after.

## What we did

We treated one wedding as what it actually is: a season's worth of marketing material. From the photos and a few notes about the brief, we built the assets that turn one booking into the next.

- A written case study — the story of the day, the flowers and the brief, ready to send to enquiring couples
- A carousel built for the way couples actually browse florists on Instagram — saved, shared, sent to the group chat
- Profiles set up properly so a couple landing from a tagged photo finds a florist who clearly knows exactly what they're doing

### Sample — the case study opening

> The flowers lasted a day. The photographs should last a lot longer than that — and for a florist, they're the whole business.

> When a couple booked Foxglove & Fern for a marquee wedding near Cirencester, the brief was "like the garden wandered in." Here's how that came together — the arch, the tablescapes, and the small decisions that made it feel gathered rather than arranged.

## Why it works

Couples book the florist whose previous weddings they can see and fall for. A florist's marketing is just their past work, presented well and put where couples are looking. One wedding, written up and shown off properly, is the most persuasive advert for the next.
